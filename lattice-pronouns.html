Designing for Inclusion: Pronouns at Lattice

Not every important project is a large one. The Lattice Pronouns feature started as a hack week initiative and shipped to production in October 2021. My role was focused on one specific and critical design problem: how and where pronouns should be displayed across a product with tens of thousands of users.

Context

The Pronouns feature originated as a Lattice hack week project — driven by engineers, product managers, and designers who cared about building a more inclusive platform. Early discovery interviews and internal enthusiasm showed real value, and the feature was added to the Platform team's roadmap for the subsequent quarter.

The work was divided between teams. The Identity team owned the data model and the editing UI — how users set and update their pronouns in account settings and during registration. My focus was the display layer: how and where pronouns should appear across the product so that they were visible when they actually mattered.

"As a manager, knowing what pronouns people identify themselves with is essential. Building trust is foundational to any manager-report relationship. It will be tough for me to build trust if I don't consider the core attributes of my teammates' identities."

Joan Roig, Engineering Manager at Lattice
The design problem

Where pronouns appear matters as much as whether they appear

Pronouns are most valuable when they're visible at the moment someone is being referred to — when writing feedback, conducting a review, nominating a peer. Surfacing them in the wrong places would create noise; missing the right places would make the feature fail its purpose.

The challenge was mapping this across a product with many surfaces — feedback tools, reviews, goals, 1:1s, people directories — and identifying which contexts were genuinely high-stakes moments where a colleague would want to refer to someone by name.

Design decisions

Consistent display without being intrusive

The input pattern used an autocomplete control — making it accessible and open-ended, since any finite list of pronouns would inevitably exclude someone. Unlike other employee attributes that admins can edit, pronouns are exclusively editable by the employee themselves. That design decision was intentional: it's not about putting people into boxes, it's about creating space for every individual to define who they are.

For display, the Identity team built a shared pronouns component that all product teams could use — so that once pronouns were fetched from the GraphQL layer, rendering them consistently was straightforward. My role included defining the display rules: where the component should appear, how it should be positioned relative to names, and how it should adapt across different layout contexts.

Design principles

Three principles guided the display decisions across the product.

Show up at the right moment

Pronouns appear in contexts where a colleague is being referred to directly — feedback, reviews, goal collaborators, people profiles. Not in navigation or administrative views where the user isn't relevant to the task.

Employee-controlled, always

Unlike other profile attributes, pronouns can only be set and changed by the employee themselves — never by an admin. The feature is about self-definition, not categorization.

Consistent across teams

A shared display component meant all product teams could implement pronouns correctly without each solving the same visual and accessibility problems independently. Consistency was baked in by design.

What I learned

This project is a reminder that design impact isn't proportional to project size. The display decisions for pronouns were relatively contained — but getting them right meant the difference between a feature that genuinely helped people feel seen in the product and one that felt like an afterthought.

It also reinforced how much design systems thinking matters for features that touch the whole product. Building a shared display component that all teams could use wasn't just an engineering convenience — it was what made the feature coherent and trustworthy at scale.
