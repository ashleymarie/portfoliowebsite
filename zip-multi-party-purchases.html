Multi-Party Purchases

Enterprise software is rarely bought directly. Resellers, marketplaces, and distributors routinely sit between buyers and the tools they actually use — but Zip's purchase model assumed a single vendor on every request. I led this design end to end: establishing the data model, validating it with customers, and implementing the full experience across every surface in the product.

Hero image Recommended: 2400 × 1030px — composite of the tabbed dual-vendor RDP, the intake chat prototype, and the vendor record relationships tab

The problem

Zip was built on a simple assumption: one purchase request, one vendor. For most purchases, that's accurate enough. But enterprise software procurement is rarely that clean. A company buying Snowflake often pays AWS Marketplace. An organization contracting an individual contributor often pays the staffing agency. A vendor often routes payments through a third-party processor neither party chose. In each case, the entity you pay and the entity you're actually buying from are different — and Zip had no way to represent that relationship.

Teams adapted with workarounds: duplicate requests, freetext comments, manual corrections by procurement after the fact. These weren't just friction points. As Zip moved upmarket they created real compliance and auditability gaps — the procurement system didn't reflect how money was actually moving.

Teams I worked across: Procurement Product Group · Requester Team · Platform Team
My role

Lead designer across a multi-team initiative

I drove this initiative end to end as the sole designer — from defining the problem space through M1 implementation. What made this work Staff-level wasn't just the design complexity, it was the organizational complexity. Multi-party purchases touch nearly every surface in the product, which meant working alongside multiple product teams simultaneously and maintaining coherence across all of them without hierarchical authority over any.

That required being the person who could see the whole system while everyone else was heads-down on their piece — and making the right calls early on scope, sequencing, and approach so the teams around me could move fast without fragmenting the experience.

The complexity

What made this harder than it looked was that "multi-party purchase" means something different to every enterprise customer. After mapping the problem space, three distinct customer models emerged — each with legitimate but incompatible assumptions about how reseller relationships should work.

These weren't configuration options — they were fundamentally different operating models. A design that worked cleanly for one would break for another. Every decision in M0 and M1 had to hold up across the full spectrum.

Approach

Ground the model first. Then implement at depth.

Rather than designing across all surfaces at once, we structured the work into phases. M0 was deliberately constrained — prove the core data model works, validate it with customers, and secure a commitment. M1 was where the real surface-area complexity hit: every intake surface, every edge case, the full procure-to-pay lifecycle. M2 will complete the picture with spend attribution and relationship lifecycle management.

M0: Grounding the model

M0 established the foundational data model: a purchase request could now carry two vendors — a service provider (the entity you're buying from) and a payee (the entity you're paying through). Before any UI work, I mapped what that model implied at every level: how vendor portals would split, how approvals would route, how the data would flow to ERP.

Three design decisions defined M0. First, a tabbed vendor section on the request detail page — one tab per vendor, each with its own portal send status — kept the relationship visible throughout the workflow without collapsing both parties into a single ambiguous view. Second, relationships are additive only: once a reseller is linked to a request, that association cannot be removed. This was a deliberate data integrity decision, not a constraint — auditability of procurement decisions requires permanence. Third, the reseller's vendor portal is scoped to payment details, documents, and surveys only. Compliance information flows to the service provider directly.

The outcome of M0 was customer validation and a strategic enterprise commitment that gave the team a real deadline. That constraint was valuable — it forced scope discipline and confirmed the model worked before we committed to full implementation.

M1 — The intake question

One question. Three surfaces. Zero admin configuration.

M0 left a gap: reseller assignment happened post-intake, after procurement reviewed the request. M1 needed to move that decision earlier — to the requester, at the moment of submission. The constraint was that Zip's intake experience exists in three forms: a traditional multi-step form, an AI chat experience, and an emerging agentic intake flow. A solution that only worked for one would create divergence across the product.

Three paths were on the table. A conditional form question admins could configure. An AI-only suggestion for customers with AI enabled. Or a hybrid approach using server-side signals — prior purchase history, existing vendor relationships — that works universally, with no admin setup required. We chose the hybrid.

The deciding factor wasn't technical complexity. It was adoption. A standalone question admins had to discover and configure would follow the same pattern as every optional intake field before it — adopted slowly, unevenly, late. An inline follow-up within the existing vendor question, triggered automatically when signals are present, reaches every customer from day one.

M1 — AI confidence design

The system should only speak when it has something useful to say

The inline prompt doesn't always appear. It fires based on signal strength — prior purchase history, existing vendor relationships on record. When signals are strong, it asserts with reasoning: "You've purchased Snowflake through AWS Marketplace before — would you like to do the same here?" When there's no signal, the UI stays silent. A prompt with no useful context would train requesters to ignore it.

While pressure-testing the Platform team's implementation, I found they hadn't fully designed for the confidence spectrum — the early build treated all signals identically, with no differentiation between high-confidence assertions and low-confidence suggestions. Correction mattered too: suggestion chips that expand to alternative channels communicate implicitly that a different answer is reachable, without surfacing technical uncertainty to the user.

M1 — Cross-team discovery

Finding scope that wasn't on anyone's roadmap

While working through the intake implementation with the Platform team, I discovered a gap that wasn't on any team's radar. Enabling the reseller prompt requires admins to configure both an intake question and an AI chat skill. But today, skills start from a blank slate — no templates, no guidance, no indication of what a well-formed skill looks like for a procurement use case.

Customers don't know how to write AI skills. A blank configuration screen is functionally a feature that won't get used — the same adoption logic that drove the inline intake decision applies here too. I'm working with the Platform team to define a templating structure so the reseller skill arrives pre-built: admins can deploy it immediately without authoring anything from scratch.

M1 — Admin configuration

Configuring portal behavior for two reseller models

Not all resellers play the same role. Some are payees only — they receive payment but have no active role in compliance or onboarding. Others are active parties who complete their own portal tasks alongside the service provider. The admin configuration needed to support both models without forcing every customer to understand the distinction before they encounter it.

In the workflow builder, admins configure portal defaults per vendor role — which portal sections a reseller can access, and whether they're engaged as a payee or an active party. Buyers can override these defaults at send time. These screens are currently being finalized.

What's next

Admin configuration screens are being finalized and the skill templating work with the Platform team is in early definition. Together these close the last gap in the M1 experience — ensuring the system works not just for requesters, but for the admins configuring it.

M2 will complete the picture: reseller-level renewal aggregation, spend attribution that separates OEM spend from reseller spend, and relationship lifecycle management — active/inactive status, preferred reseller designation — so that the data model established in M0 can fully power Zip's enterprise reporting capabilities.

What I learned

The hardest call on this project wasn't a design decision — it was recognizing that adoption risk and technical risk are different things that require different responses. The standalone intake question was the technically lower-risk option: more modular, easier to scope, easier to test in isolation. But it would have required every admin to discover, understand, and add a new question to their workflow. Evidence from prior feature rollouts said that doesn't happen fast or broadly enough to matter. Choosing the inline approach meant taking on more technical complexity in exchange for eliminating the adoption bottleneck entirely. That tradeoff only became visible because we looked at rollout data from past features — not just the engineering considerations directly in front of us.

The other lesson was about the value of going deep enough to find scope that isn't in anyone's brief. The skill templating gap, the confidence-level logic the Platform team had missed — neither were on a roadmap. They surfaced because I was close enough to the implementation to see them. At Staff level, that's part of the job: not just executing the defined work, but noticing what the defined work missed.
