Supplier Relationships at Enterprise Scale

Zip's supplier model was built for simplicity — one request, one vendor. As Zip moved upmarket, that model broke under the weight of real enterprise complexity. I drove this initiative end-to-end as the sole designer, spanning five product teams and seven surfaces.

Hero image Recommended: 2400 × 1030px — a wide composite of the key screens

The problem

Large enterprise customers operate with intricate vendor hierarchies: parent-child relationships, resellers, OEMs, contractor-agency structures, and multiple ERP-mapped supplier sites. Every customer has a distinct ERP setup — Oracle, SAP, and others — and how they ingest vendors directly shapes how relationships need to be modeled in Zip.

The business signals were unambiguous: lost deals, churn risk from existing enterprise customers hitting hard limits, and rising support volume around vendor management.

Teams I worked across: Procurement Product Group · Requester Team · Approvals Team · Contract Orchestration · Advanced Payments
My role

Staff-level design across an entire product ecosystem

I drove this initiative end-to-end as the sole designer — from defining the problem space with customers and leadership through to launch. What made this Staff-level wasn't just the design complexity, it was the organizational scope. Vendor relationships touch nearly every surface in the product, which meant designing alongside four separate product teams simultaneously, ensuring what each team shipped connected into one coherent experience.

Approach

Mapping the system before designing any of it

Rather than diving into design, I started by mapping every product surface that vendor relationships touched. This cross-team map became the shared foundation all five teams aligned around — without it, each team would have solved their piece in isolation and shipped a fragmented experience.

I also advocated early for an AI-powered intake question. Engineering pushed back on M0 scope; I held the position as customer evidence kept building. We ultimately designed and shipped it integrated with the Requester team's new AI capabilities.

What I learned

The hardest part of Staff-level design isn't the design itself — it's maintaining a coherent vision across teams that each have their own priorities, timelines, and constraints. On this project that meant being the person who could see the whole system when everyone else was heads-down on their piece, and making enough of the right calls early — on scope, on sequencing, on the intake question — that teams around me could move fast without fragmenting the experience.
