Supplier Relationships at Enterprise Scale

Zip's request model assumed every purchase was simple: one team, one vendor, one payment. As Zip moved upmarket, that assumption became its #1 customer pain — and I led the redesign that fixed it, end-to-end across five product teams and seven surfaces.

Background

Zip is a procurement orchestration platform: the system companies use to request, approve, and pay for everything they buy, giving procurement and finance teams a single place to manage suppliers, spend, and budgets. One construct shaped that system from the start: one request = one supplier = one payment. That simplicity is what made Zip fast to adopt, but it carried a hidden assumption — that the supplier on the request is always the supplier who gets paid.

As Zip moved upmarket, that assumption broke. The clearest case was software bought through a reseller, where the vendor a team contracts with often isn't the vendor that's ultimately paid. The same shape showed up everywhere once we looked, from agencies working through contractors to OEMs working through suppliers. A request was still tied to a single supplier, but who gets requested and who gets paid were increasingly two different things. It became Zip's #1 customer pain.

My role

As design lead for the Supplier team, I drove this initiative end-to-end, from defining the problem space with customers and leadership through to launch. My goal wasn't just to support these relationships, but to make sure the expanded capability was considered across the entire supplier lifecycle and every surface it touched.

Because vendor relationships reach nearly every part of the product, the work extended well beyond my own team. I was hands-on designing solutions across multiple product areas at once and coordinating closely with the teams that own them so that every change fit their surface and the direction they were already heading. The hardest part was the breadth it demanded: understanding the entire product deeply enough to trace how multi-party purchases would ripple into each area, and getting ahead of those impacts before they became gaps.

Teams I worked across: Procurement Product Group · Requester Team · Approvals Team · Contract Orchestration · Advanced Payments

The problem

Large enterprise customers operate with intricate vendor hierarchies — parent-child relationships, resellers, OEMs, contractor-agency structures, and multiple ERP-mapped supplier sites. Every customer also runs a distinct ERP setup (Oracle, SAP, and others), and how they ingest vendors on their side directly shapes how relationships need to be modeled in Zip.

The business signals were unambiguous: lost deals, churn risk from enterprise customers hitting hard limits, and rising support volume around vendor management.

Underneath the business case was a design tension. These new relationships added complexity to a system that was already dense. The real challenge wasn't just supporting them — it was doing so while keeping the experience light, intuitive, and even delightful.

The approach

Three moves shaped the work — mapping every surface vendor relationships touched, defining purchase channels as the model that unified them, and advocating for an AI-assisted intake so requesters never have to know the difference.

Outcomes

This work moved Zip from a product that could demo enterprise procurement to one that could actually support it at scale — directly unlocking deals and expansions that weren't possible before.

Unlocked enterprise deals and expansions, including initial deployment with a major enterprise customer
Design coherence maintained across 5 product teams and 7 surfaces simultaneously — without hierarchical authority over any of them
Established the vendor relationship foundation that Zip's entire upmarket strategy now builds on

What's next

The full surface mapping didn't just shape the design — it shaped the roadmap. With every affected area visible, my PM and I deliberately scoped the highest-impact, most-needed work into the first release. From there we planned M1 as a fast follow, extending multi-party purchases into the contract and invoice layers. Because purchase channels already exist as shared infrastructure, that release builds on the model rather than reopening it.

What I learned

When work spans this many surfaces, it's easy for a detail to slip through the cracks. The job became being the person who could see the whole system while everyone else was heads-down on their piece — and making enough of the right calls early, on scope, on sequencing, on the intake question, that the teams around me could move fast without fragmenting the experience.

That's the difference between shipping a feature and establishing a foundation: the model we defined here is what Zip's upmarket strategy keeps building on.
