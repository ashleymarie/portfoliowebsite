Helping Customers Stay in the Know

Vendr's notification system had grown organically across email and Slack without a coherent strategy — templates were redundant, content was inconsistent, and users had no centralized way to manage their preferences. This project overhauled the entire system, introduced in-app notifications for the first time, and established the standards that six product teams now build on.

Hero image Recommended: 2400 × 1030px — composite of email, in-app, and settings designs

The problem

For a product built around keeping buyers informed — renewals, approvals, tasks, contract deadlines — broken notifications meant broken trust. Six product teams were each handling notifications independently, which meant no consistency, no shared standards, and no visibility into what was being sent across the product.

Users were receiving inconsistent, sometimes redundant messages across email and Slack with no way to manage their preferences in one place. There was no in-app notification experience at all. And with no catalog of what was being sent, the team had no way to know the scale of the problem before this project began.

Approach

Auditing the full system before designing anything

My team and I took a multi-pronged approach to understanding the current state. We audited all existing email and Slack templates including their content, analyzed customer feedback, and organized sessions with each of the six product teams to understand their current and future notification needs.

We cataloged every notification and its channels in one place — a process that also aligned with migrating our notifications infrastructure to Courier, a third-party tool that gave us proper visibility and tracking across what was being sent.

Design direction

Competitive analysis and cross-team alignment

I completed a competitive analysis of notification control centers, messaging patterns, and notification types across popular SaaS products. I then paired with designers from each of the other product teams to look at their notification designs and align on patterns and best practices that would work across the broadest range of use cases.

This cross-team design alignment was as important as the visual design work itself — without it, each team would have continued solving notifications independently and the same inconsistencies would have returned.

What we shipped

01
Updated email & Slack designs

Readable, actionable, and consistently following best practices across all templates — replacing the fragmented designs each team had shipped independently.

02
In-app notification experience

Implemented using Courier's inbox — giving users a centralized place to receive and review notifications in-app for the first time. A common customer request finally addressed.

03
Updated navigation

Surfaced the notification inbox in the product's main nav — making it discoverable without disrupting the existing information architecture.

04
Content design standards

Aligned on notification content design across all teams and documented the standards in the design system — the infrastructure that makes future consistency possible without central oversight.

05
Redesigned notification settings center

A robust, updated settings experience giving users full control over their preferences by channel — opt-in/out at the category level, with sensible defaults built in.

Impact

Built to be democratized. By establishing shared standards and migrating to Courier, we gave all six product teams the foundation to iterate on notifications independently — without creating new inconsistencies.

What I learned

The most valuable thing this project produced wasn't the in-app notification experience — it was the standards. By democratizing the notification system and giving each product team clear guidelines, we made it possible for the whole org to iterate on notifications without creating new inconsistencies.

That's the difference between solving a problem and building infrastructure that prevents the problem from recurring. The 74% settings engagement on launch was gratifying — but the fact that six teams can now ship notifications independently, consistently, is the outcome that scales.
