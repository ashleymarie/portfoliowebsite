Reviews: From Strategy to Execution

Lattice's Reviews product was the company's tent-pole product, but its setup and configuration experience hadn't kept pace with how the product had grown. I led this initiative across both halves: from the research and concept strategy that defined what the product needed to become, through to the complete redesign that shipped it.

Background

Reviews was Lattice's tent-pole product — the experience organizations depend on for performance and compensation decisions. But its setup and configuration experience hadn't kept pace with how the product had grown, accumulating UX debt as it scaled.

Admins — the champions and advocates of Lattice at their organizations — were navigating a fragmented, overloaded interface to configure review cycles that only happen one to two times per year. The stakes were high: a confusing setup experience meant support tickets, churn risk, and erosion of trust in a product organizations rely on.

My role

I led this initiative across both halves — from the research and concept strategy that defined what the product needed to become, through to the complete redesign that shipped it. Leading both strategy and execution on a newly formed team meant defining the work and building the team's ways of working at the same time.

Throughout, I partnered closely with my product partner on research, with engineering on the underlying information architecture, and piloted Lattice's burgeoning content design practice by pairing with their first content design hire across execution.

The problem

The Reviews setup process had accumulated UX debt as the product scaled. Information was scattered across multiple pages. Important decisions carried the same visual weight as minor ones. Admins had to hold the entire flow in their heads while configuring — with no way to see how choices across pages connected to each other.

Three guiding problem statements emerged from research and framed the work:

How do I match my current review process to a reviews tool? · How do I understand each option and configuration in Lattice and make sure it aligns with what I want my reviews process to be? · How do I run reviews effectively?

The approach

The work moved through three phases — researching where setup broke down, designing and validating concepts to find the right direction, then executing a complete redesign grounded in a new information architecture.

What shipped

A complete redesign of the review cycle creation experience across more than 11 distinct pages, organized around Setup by Direction — the winning concept from testing. Rather than a flat list of settings scattered across pages, the experience reorganized around review directions — downward, upward, peer, self — grouping related settings so dependencies were immediately visible. This wasn't a visual refresh; it was a rethinking of the underlying information architecture to match how admins actually think about reviews.

Outcomes

Before launch, we established a CSAT baseline with the data team and monitored it closely post-launch. The results reflected the investment made upfront in getting the strategy right before building.

22% increase in Admin CSAT — measured against a baseline established before launch
Meaningful reduction in time spent by Customer Support assisting customers with review creation
Established the foundation for the Reviews Admin team's future roadmap
Piloted Lattice's content design practice — a model that extended to other teams

What I learned

The strategy half of this project was as important as the execution half — maybe more so. Investing in eight concepts and 11 rounds of testing before committing to a direction meant that when we built, we built the right thing. The 22% CSAT lift wasn't luck — it was the result of knowing exactly what problem we were solving before touching a single production screen.

Leading both strategy and execution on a newly formed team simultaneously also meant defining the work and building the team's ways of working at the same time. That's a different kind of challenge than either alone.
